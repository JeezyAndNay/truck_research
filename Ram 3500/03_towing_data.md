# Ram 3500 HD — Towing Data
*6.7L Cummins I6 · SRW · Crew Cab · 4x4*
*Research date: June 2026*

---

> **How to read towing charts:** Published max ratings are always for the most favorable configuration under ideal conditions — correct axle ratio, max tow package, right transmission. Your actual truck may rate lower. The door jamb sticker on your specific truck is the only number that matters for your rig.

## What Actually Determines Your Rating

### Axle Ratio
The single biggest variable. The 4.10 axle is what gets the Ram 3500 to its published max. The 3.73 (more common on lower trims) reduces towing and payload capacity meaningfully.

- **4.10 axle:** Higher towing capacity, better performance under load, slightly lower fuel economy
- **3.73 axle:** Better highway fuel economy, noticeably less tow capacity
- **Recommendation:** For towing a 10,000–14,000 lb travel trailer, either works fine. For maximum headroom, target 4.10 axle on a used buy.

### Max Tow Package
Required to unlock max ratings. Typically includes:
- 4.10 rear axle
- Trailer brake controller
- Heavy-duty cooling package
- Class V 2.5" receiver hitch
- Upgraded alternator

### Transmission
HO + Aisin = higher max tow rating than SO + 68RFE. Both transmissions are capable for travel trailer use in the 10,000–14,000 lb range, but the Aisin handles sustained towing heat better.

## What Matters for Your Use Case

**Travel trailer at 10,000–14,000 lbs:**

| Metric | Your Trailer | Ram 3500 (either config) | Headroom |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tow rating | 10,000–14,000 lbs | 26,000–37,090 lbs | 85–165% remaining |
| 5th wheel | N/A (conventional) | N/A | — |
| Payload | Pin weight + gear | 3,000–3,500 lbs SRW | Watch this one |

**Payload is the real constraint, not tow rating.** For a conventional travel trailer, you need the tongue weight (typically 10–15% of trailer weight) to fit within the truck's payload rating — along with passengers, fuel, and cargo.

*Example: 14,000 lb trailer @ 13% tongue weight = 1,820 lb tongue weight. Add 2 people (400 lbs), fuel (200 lbs), gear (300 lbs) = ~2,720 lbs against a ~3,000–3,500 lb payload. You're fine, but check the door jamb sticker on your specific truck.*

## GCWR (Gross Combined Weight Rating)

| Engine | GCWR |
|---|---|
| Cummins HO | ~43,000 lbs |
| Cummins SO | ~37,000 lbs |

GCWR = truck weight + trailer weight + payload. Must not exceed this number. For a fully loaded Ram 3500 HO (7,200 lbs) + 14,000 lb trailer = 21,200 lbs — well within GCWR.

## Hitch Receiver

- **Class V 2.5" receiver** standard on 3500 HD with Max Tow Package
- **Ball mount:** Drop/rise depending on trailer coupler height. Measure coupler-to-ground and hitch-to-ground.
- **Weight distribution hitch:** Not required for conventional trailer at 10,000–14,000 lbs in a 3500, but can improve handling and level the truck. Optional, not mandatory.
- **Brake controller:** Required by law in most states for trailers over 3,000 lbs. Built-in on most 3500 trims.

---

## Real-World Towing Notes

- **Cummins exhaust brake:** Built-in on all diesel Ram 3500s. Outstanding for mountain descents with a loaded trailer. Highly effective — reduces brake fade on long grades.
- **Air suspension (Limited/Longhorn):** Raises ride height and levels the truck under load. Helps with tongue weight sag. Not a substitute for a proper weight distribution setup if severely overloaded.
- **Trailer sway control:** Integrated with stability control. Works with trailer brake controller. Keep trailer loaded with 60% of weight forward of trailer axle.
- **Fuel economy towing:** Expect 8–12 MPG depending on trailer drag, grade, and speed. 65 mph vs 70 mph makes a meaningful difference on long trips.
