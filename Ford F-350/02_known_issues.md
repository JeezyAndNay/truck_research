# Ford F-350 HD — Known Issues by Year
*6.7L Power Stroke V8 · SRW · Crew Cab · 4x4*
*Research date: June 2026*

---

> **CP4 warning — all years:** Every 6.7L Power Stroke from 2011 to present uses the Bosch CP4.2 injection pump. There is no year or trim level that avoids it. When it fails, it contaminates the entire high-pressure fuel system. Cost: $8,000–$20,000. This is the single most important used-buying factor for any F-350.

## 2011 — First Year (Most Issues)

The 6.7L Power Stroke debuted in 2011, replacing the 6.4L. First-year production had several documented problems that were addressed in subsequent model years.

### 🔴 CP4.2 Injection Pump — Present on All 6.7L
- CP4.2 present from day one on the 6.7L Power Stroke
- US diesel fuel's low sulfur content means inadequate pump lubrication
- **Failure mode:** Metal shrapnel into injectors, rails, lines, fuel tank
- **Cost of failure:** $8,000–$20,000
- Ford class-action settlements (Speerly v. Ford) have resulted in extended warranty coverage for many CP4 failures on 2011–2019 F-250/F-350
- **Preventive measure:** FASS or AirDog fuel system (~$600–$1,200 installed). Many experienced F-350 owners treat this as mandatory on any used purchase.

### 🔴 Turbocharger Failure — Ceramic Ball Bearings *(2011, early production)*
- Early 2011 production Honeywell DualBoost turbos used ceramic ball bearings that failed prematurely
- Symptoms: obnoxious screeching noise, blue/black smoke, sudden power loss
- Ford transitioned to steel ball bearings in later 2011 production and 2012+
- **Serial number / build date check:** If buying a 2011, verify which turbo bearing design is installed
- **Fix:** Turbo replacement (~$2,500–$4,000 installed). Remanufactured turbos with steel bearings are the correct remedy.

### 🟠 NOx Sensor Failure *(2011)*
- "Extremely common" on 2011 model year — Ford's own service data acknowledged this
- Ford issued Customer Satisfaction Program **12B33** covering NOx sensor replacements through April 2013
- Symptoms: reduced power mode, fault codes, check engine light
- **Fix:** NOx sensor replacement (~$200–$500). If 2011, verify CSP 12B33 was performed.

### 🟠 Turbo Coolant Inlet Fitting Leak *(2011–2016)*
- A plastic coolant fitting on the turbocharger coolant feed line cracks and leaks
- Loss of coolant, potential overheating
- Affects 2011–2016 across both generations of the 6.7L
- **Fix:** Billet aluminum replacement fitting (~$40–$80 + labor). Easy DIY fix; prevents significant damage.

### 🟡 Glow Plug Tip Break-Off *(early production 2011)*
- Some early 2011 glow plugs had tips break off in the cylinder head
- Mostly isolated to chassis cab models (F-450, F-550 Ambulance Package trucks)
- No widespread recall; isolated incidents
- **Fix:** Professional glow plug removal (risk of head damage if broken) — preventive replacement at 100K miles is cheaper

### ✅ EGT Sensor Failure — Chassis Cab Recall *(2011–2012)*
- Ford issued **recall 13S10** for EGT (exhaust gas temperature) sensor failures on F-350/F-450/F-550 chassis cabs with "Ambulance Package"
- Could cause engine shutdown or restart failure
- Standard crew cab F-350s less affected; recall was narrow in scope

## 2012–2014 — First Generation, Improved

Ford addressed the 2011 launch issues during 2012–2014 production. More stable but still inheriting the core architecture risks.

### 🟠 EGR Cooler Failure *(2011–2016, common on 2012–2014)*
- EGR cooler is a known weakness on the 6.7L Power Stroke
- High EGR flow rates deposit soot in the cooler; coolant passages crack over time
- Symptoms: white smoke (coolant in exhaust), coolant loss, overheating
- Codes: P0401 (EGR insufficient flow), coolant temp codes
- **Fix:** EGR cooler replacement ($600–$1,400 parts + labor). Some owners do a coolant flush at the same time.

### 🟠 Turbo Coolant Inlet Fitting *(ongoing 2012–2016)*
- Same plastic fitting issue from 2011 continues through first-gen production
- **Fix:** Same billet replacement fitting — easy and cheap

### 🟡 IWE (Integrated Wheel End) 4WD Actuator Failure *(2013–2016)*
- The IWE system engages front hub locks for 4WD via vacuum
- Vacuum line failures and actuator failures cause grinding noise in 4WD or refusal to engage
- Very common complaint across all Super Duty years from 2013 onward
- **Fix:** IWE actuator replacement, vacuum line repair (~$200–$600). Many owners install a vacuum check valve to prevent backflow issues.

### 🟡 Fuel Dilution / "Making Oil" *(all years)*
- DPF active regeneration injects raw diesel into the exhaust stream
- In stop-and-go driving, fuel can enter the crankcase, diluting engine oil
- Oil level rises above max on the dipstick; oil smells of diesel
- **Fix:** More frequent oil changes (every 5,000 miles in heavy regen cycles). This is a design reality of DPF-equipped diesels, not a "fixable" defect. Highway use minimizes regen frequency.

## 2015–2016 — Second Generation Power Stroke

2015 was a significant update to the 6.7L. New pistons, improved EGR, updated turbocharger. Higher output (440 hp / 860 lb-ft vs. 400 hp / 800 lb-ft). Still CP4.2.

### 🔴 CP4.2 *(continues — still every 6.7L)*
- No change to injection pump design in 2015
- The Speerly v. Ford class-action settlement covers some 2011–2019 failures — check VIN eligibility

### 🟠 EGR Cooler *(improved but not eliminated)*
- Second-gen EGR cooler design improved flow — fewer early failures vs. first gen
- Still fails eventually, especially under heavy towing + frequent regen cycles
- **Fix:** Same as 2011–2014 ($600–$1,400)

### 🟠 Fan Clutch Failure *(2015–2019)*
- Fan clutch failures became more common starting in 2015
- Symptoms: engine overheating, loud fan roar, or fan not engaging at temp
- **Fix:** Fan clutch replacement (~$400–$900 installed)

### 🟠 Crankshaft Vibration Damper Failure *(2014–2019)*
- Crank damper delamination causes severe vibration and potential belt/pulley damage if it fails completely
- Common enough that this is a known maintenance item, not a fluke
- Symptoms: belt slapping, harmonic vibration, front-end noise at idle
- **Fix:** Harmonic balancer/damper replacement (~$300–$700 parts + labor). Check at every oil change above 100K miles.

### 🟡 IWE 4WD Actuator *(ongoing)*
- Same issue continues through second generation
- **Fix:** Same as above

---

## 2017–2019 — Third Generation Power Stroke

2017 brought the third evolution of the 6.7L: new injectors, updated turbo, higher output (450 hp / 935 lb-ft). Still CP4.2.

### 🔴 CP4.2 *(still all 6.7L)*
- 2017+ trucks are now aging into the highest-risk window for CP4 failure
- Many used 2017–2019s are in the 80,000–150,000 mile range where CP4 failure accelerates
- **Recommendation:** Do not buy a 2017–2019 F-350 without either (a) an aftermarket fuel filtration system already installed, or (b) significant price negotiation to account for the risk

### 🟠 EGR Cooler *(3rd gen, still recurring)*
- Third-gen Power Stroke still sees EGR cooler failures, particularly on trucks with heavy regen cycles
- Some improvement in design, but not eliminated

### 🟠 High-Pressure Fuel Pump Cover Bolts *(2017–2019)*
- CP4 pump cover bolts can back out and cause fuel leaks
- Rare but documented; inspect during pre-purchase inspection
- **Fix:** Thread repair or replacement bolts with Loctite

### 🟡 Fan Clutch *(ongoing 2017–2019)*
- Continues from second generation

### 🟡 Crankshaft Damper *(ongoing 2017–2019)*
- Higher mileage 2017s now entering the risk window

---

## 2020–2022 — Fourth Generation Power Stroke

2020 brought the fourth-generation 6.7L (475 hp / 1,050 lb-ft) and the new 10-speed TorqShift transmission.

### 🔴 CP4.2 *(still all 6.7L — fourth generation)*
- Fourth gen still CP4.2
- No change to fuel pump architecture despite class-action pressure

### 🟠 10-Speed TorqShift Shift Quality *(2020–2021)*
- First-year 10-speed TorqShift (6R140 → 10R140) had shift programming issues
- Harsh downshifts, hunt-and-peck under moderate load
- Ford released multiple transmission software updates (TSBs)
- **Recommendation:** Confirm TSB updates have been applied on any 2020–2021 purchase

### 🟠 EGR Cooler *(continues)*
- Even 4th-gen Power Stroke trucks are not immune to EGR cooler failure
- Timeline to first failure has extended vs. 1st gen, but not eliminated

### 🟡 DEF System Sensor Issues *(2020+)*
- DEF quality sensor failures causing false fault codes
- **Fix:** Sensor replacement (~$200–$500)

---

## 2023–2024 — Current Production

### 🟠 CP4.2 *(still present)*
- Ford has not moved away from CP4.2 as of 2024
- Current trucks have better CP4 manufacturing tolerances but the fundamental design vulnerability remains
- Most buyers of new 2023–2024 F-350 are adding aftermarket fuel filtration from day one

### 🟡 All previous recurring items
- EGR cooler, IWE actuator, fuel dilution — all still present in current production

---

## CP4 Class Action — What It Means for Buyers

The Speerly v. Ford Motor Company class-action lawsuit was settled and resulted in Ford extending coverage for CP4 failures on certain affected VINs. Key points:

- Covered trucks: **2011–2019 F-250 and F-350** with 6.7L Power Stroke
- If a CP4 failure occurred within a coverage period, Ford may reimburse repair costs
- **Check your VIN** for settlement eligibility at the class-action administrator's site or through a Ford dealer
- Coverage does NOT protect future owners unconditionally — the settlement had specific terms

> For a used F-350 purchase: ask the seller for any CP4 documentation (prior failure, lawsuit remedy applied, fuel filtration added). No documentation = assume worst case.

## Summary: Worst Years vs. Best Years

| Category | Worst Years | Best Years |
|---|---|---|
| Turbo reliability | 2011 (ceramic bearings) | 2012+ (steel bearings) |
| EGR cooler | 2011–2014 (first gen worst) | 2017+ (improved) |
| CP4 risk | All years (no escape) | — |
| Transmission | 2020–2021 (early 10-speed bugs) | 2022+ (TSBs applied) |
| Overall | 2011 (most issues) | 2017–2019 (best pre-10-spd) |

**Best used buy:** 2017–2019 with CP4 filtration already installed and documented service history. The 3rd-gen Power Stroke is the strongest first-gen F-350 buy if you've done your CP4 homework.

**Avoid:** 2011 without verification of turbo bearing type and NOx CSP completion. Any truck with undocumented CP4 history.

**Non-negotiable:** Budget for or require an existing FASS/AirDog fuel system on any used F-350 purchase.
